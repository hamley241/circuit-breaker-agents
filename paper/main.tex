\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as compositional systems in which multiple agents interact to solve complex tasks. While such architectures enable modularity and improved capability, they introduce a new class of failure modes: errors originating in one component can propagate through the system, leading to cascade failures.

We study failure propagation in multi-agent LLM pipelines and evaluate circuit breakers as a mechanism for containment. We introduce Cascade Failure Rate (CFR) and provide both simulation and real-world validation.

Our results show that up to 62\% of upstream failures propagate to final failure without protection, while circuit breakers can eliminate cascade failures entirely. We further show that adaptive containment strategies introduce a tradeoff between failure containment and task success.

We extend this analysis and show that this tradeoff is not inherent to circuit breaking, but is induced by degradation in fallback performance under load. When fallback quality remains high, adaptive strategies dominate; degradation introduces the observed tradeoff.

These findings suggest that reliability in compositional LLM systems is governed not only by component-level accuracy but by the dynamics of failure propagation and fallback behavior.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-agent LLM systems are increasingly used for planning, tool use, and reasoning. These systems improve capability but introduce new failure modes.

A key challenge is failure propagation: errors introduced in early stages can cascade through downstream components. Unlike isolated errors, these failures compound across stages.

Failure propagation scales multiplicatively with pipeline depth, making system reliability fundamentally different from component-level reliability.

We frame compositional LLM systems as distributed systems where reliability depends on controlling error propagation. Inspired by circuit breakers, we study mechanisms to contain failures before they cascade.

A central question is whether containment mechanisms introduce unavoidable tradeoffs. Prior observations suggest that reducing cascade failures comes at the cost of reduced task success. In this work, we revisit this assumption and show that the tradeoff emerges from fallback degradation rather than being inherent.

\section{Theoretical Framework}

Let $p_u$ denote upstream failure probability and $p_p$ denote propagation probability.

\begin{equation}
P(\text{final failure}) = p_u \cdot p_p
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\text{CFR} = P(\text{final failure} \mid \text{upstream failure})
\end{equation}

For a pipeline of length $L$:

\begin{equation}
\text{CFR}(L) = 1 - (1 - p_p)^L
\end{equation}

Even moderate propagation probabilities lead to high end-to-end failure rates in deeper pipelines. Circuit breakers reduce effective propagation by interrupting failure chains.

\section{Method}

We evaluate three configurations: no circuit breaker, AI-based circuit breaker, and adaptive circuit breaker.

We use a planner–executor–verifier pipeline. Failures are injected according to upstream probability $p_u$, and propagate with probability $p_p$.

An upstream failure is defined as invalid, incomplete, or inconsistent output.

We extend the model to include fallback behavior. When a breaker triggers, execution is routed to a fallback with success probability:

\begin{equation}
s_f = s_0 (1 - \alpha \cdot \text{load})
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ captures degradation strength.

\section{Experiments}

We evaluate pipelines across simulation and real-world settings.

Simulation sweeps propagation probability, degradation strength, and cost of catastrophic failure.

Real-world experiments use a planner–executor–verifier pipeline with structured outputs.

\section{Discussion}

Failure propagation is a system-level phenomenon. Reliability depends on controlling propagation and maintaining fallback quality.

\section{Related Work}

Prior work on multi-agent LLMs focuses on capability. Distributed systems introduce circuit breakers but assume binary failures. Our work models probabilistic propagation and degradation-induced tradeoffs.

\section{Conclusion}

Failure propagation governs reliability in LLM systems. Circuit breakers mitigate cascades, and adaptive policies improve utility. The safety--availability tradeoff is induced by fallback degradation.
